Explain the following quote from the Great Gatsby: 

‘She's got an indiscreet voice,’ I remarked. ‘It's full of—'
I hesitated.
‘Her voice is full of money,’ he said suddenly.
That was it. I'd never understood before. It was full of money—that was the inexhaustible charm that rose and fell in it, the jingle of it, the cymbals' song of it. . . . High in a white palace the king's daughter, the golden girl. . . .
§a§
This evocative quote from F. Scott Fitzgerald’s *The Great Gatsby* captures a pivotal moment of insight into the character of Daisy Buchanan and the novel’s central themes of wealth, illusion, and the American Dream.

When Nick Carraway remarks that Daisy has an “indiscreet voice,” he senses there’s something revealing and perhaps inappropriate in the way she speaks—something that betrays her inner world. But he can’t quite articulate what it is. Then Gatsby, with sudden clarity, says, *“Her voice is full of money.”* This line is one of the most famous in American literature because it so perfectly distills the essence of Daisy—and the world she represents.

Gatsby’s observation means that Daisy’s voice carries the tone, rhythm, and allure of wealth. It’s not just that she is rich, but that her entire manner—her speech, her laughter, her presence—is saturated with the privileges and sounds of affluence. The “jingle of it, the cymbals' song of it” suggests the musical, almost hypnotic quality of her voice, reminiscent of coins clinking or the glittering, superficial glamour of high society. Her voice doesn’t just speak words—it performs wealth.

The metaphor continues: *“High in a white palace the king's daughter, the golden girl.”* Here, Daisy is elevated to a mythic status—untouchable, regal, and made of gold. She is not just a woman but a symbol: the ultimate prize in Gatsby’s dream of upward mobility and romantic fulfillment. Her value, in Gatsby’s eyes, is inseparable from her wealth and social standing.

Yet, this description is also deeply ironic and tragic. While Gatsby idealizes Daisy as a golden goddess, the reality is that her voice—“full of money”—reveals her moral emptiness and superficiality. The charm Nick and Gatsby find so captivating is ultimately hollow, rooted in materialism rather than depth or integrity. Fitzgerald uses this moment to critique the American Dream itself: the idea that wealth and status can bring happiness or meaning. Daisy may be beautiful and enchanting, but she is also careless, self-absorbed, and ultimately incapable of the love and loyalty Gatsby imagines.

In short, the quote reveals that Daisy embodies the seductive yet corrupting power of wealth. Her voice, “full of money,” symbolizes the glittering surface of the Jazz Age—beautiful, intoxicating, but ultimately empty.
